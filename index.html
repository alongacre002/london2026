Tuesday Afternoon — tap to expand each option
🏛 National Gallery + Trafalgar Square

Walk there: Easy 15–20 min walk from Buckingham Palace via St James's Park and The Mall. One of the most pleasant walks in London.

The National Gallery is free entry and houses one of the world's greatest art collections — Van Gogh's Sunflowers, Monet's water lilies, da Vinci, Rembrandt. You don't need to be an art person to appreciate it. Allow 1–1.5 hours. The kids don't need to see everything — pick a few rooms and move on.

Trafalgar Square is right outside — one of London's great public spaces. The four giant bronze lions at the base of Nelson's Column are a must for a family photo. The fountains are beautiful and there's usually something going on. It's also the hub for buskers and street performers.

For lunch: The National Portrait Gallery is right next door and has a good café. Or grab something from one of the spots around the square and eat outside if the weather cooperates.

Good for: All three kids, though the 14 and 15 year olds will get more out of the gallery. The 11-year-old will love the lions and the square regardless.

🎭 Covent Garden

Getting there: About 20–25 min walk from Buckingham Palace, or one tube stop from Westminster or Green Park. Easy to reach from anywhere in the afternoon.

The Piazza: The heart of Covent Garden has some of the best street performers in London — acrobats, magicians, comedians, musicians. Acts perform on a raised stone platform and rotate through every 30–40 minutes. No cost, but the crowd tips at the end. The 11-year-old will be completely transfixed. Acts vary day to day but Monday through Friday afternoons are reliably busy.

The Market Building: Three floors of independent shops, art, jewellery and clothing. Good for the 14 and 15 year olds to browse at their own pace while adults grab a coffee at one of the surrounding cafés. The lower ground floor has the Apple Market with craft stalls.

Neal's Yard: A 5-minute walk north from the piazza — a tiny hidden courtyard with brightly painted buildings. Worth finding. Great photo moment and the kids will appreciate the "hidden London" feel. Head up Shorts Gardens from the market building.

Seven Dials: Just northwest of the market — a junction of seven streets lined with independent shops, vintage clothing, and bookshops. More interesting than the main tourist drag and good for the teenagers who want to feel like they're discovering something.

For lunch: Dishoom (5 min walk, Indian comfort food, always a hit with teens — expect a short queue), Shake Shack is right in the market building, or plenty of casual options in the covered market itself.

Good for: All three kids, different things for different ages. The 11-year-old for the performers, the older two for the shopping and exploring.

🎨 V&A Museum

Getting there: Tube from Green Park or Victoria → South Kensington (District/Piccadilly). The museum is a 5 min walk from the station through a covered tunnel.

The building itself is extraordinary — a Victorian palace of art and design. Even if the kids aren't natural museum-goers, walking through the grand halls is an experience. The main entrance hall alone is worth it.

Best sections for your age group:

  • Fashion galleries — centuries of clothing and costume, always a hit with teenagers
  • Medieval & Renaissance rooms — armour, jewellery, tapestries, intricate objects
  • Cast Courts — enormous hall filled with plaster casts of famous sculptures including a full-size Trajan's Column. Genuinely jaw-dropping room
  • Jewellery gallery — tiaras, gemstones, historic pieces from around the world

Natural History Museum is literally next door if the group wants to split — the 11-year-old in particular might prefer the dinosaurs and the blue whale. Both are free. You could do an hour in each.

Dinner logistics bonus: South Kensington to Fulham Broadway is one stop on the District line — useful if you ever revisit dinner options in that direction.

Good for: All ages, but especially the 14 and 15 year olds. The Cast Courts alone justify the visit.

☕ Downtime in Mayfair

After two very full days — Churchill, the Thames, Borough Market, the Tower of London, and Changing of the Guard — an afternoon off is completely legitimate. Don't feel you have to fill every hour.

Shepherd Market is the best option for a relaxed wander. It's a tiny village tucked behind Curzon Street, about 5 minutes walk from Derby St. Cobblestone lanes, independent cafés, wine bars and bistros, a few boutiques. Sit at an outdoor table, have a coffee or a glass of wine, let the kids explore the little streets. It feels nothing like tourist London and it's literally on your doorstep.

Green Park — bring a blanket and just sit. In late March the daffodils are usually out. The kids can decompress, the adults can decompress. You're back at the Airbnb in minutes whenever you're ready.

Fortnum & Mason on Piccadilly is worth 30–40 minutes if anyone has energy for a wander. The food halls are extraordinary — biscuit tins, preserves, teas, hampers. The kids usually find it fascinating in a "this is extremely British" way. Free to browse, and you can pick up tea or shortbread to take home.

Bond Street / South Molton Street — if the 14 and 15 year olds have any appetite for shopping or window shopping, this is 10 minutes walk. South Molton Street is a pedestrianised strip with a more interesting mix than the very high-end Bond Street itself.

Good for: Everyone. Sometimes the best travel moments are the unplanned ones. Save the energy for a good dinner at Ciccaro.

Wednesday Afternoon — tap to expand each option
🛍 Camden Market

Getting there: From Primrose Hill, it's a 15 min walk south down Regent's Park Road into Camden. Or tube from Camden Town station back to the area after lunch if you've wandered.

The market itself is actually a collection of several interconnected market areas — the main Camden Market, the Stables Market (the biggest and most interesting, housed in old horse stables and railway arches), and Camden Lock Market by the canal. Allow 2 hours to properly explore.

For the 14 and 15 year olds: Vintage clothing, band merch, independent music shops, street art, alternative fashion, tattoo studios to look at from the outside. This is genuinely their territory. Give them some space to explore independently — Camden is very safe and very busy.

For the 11-year-old: The food stalls, the general chaos and colour, watching the older teens react to things, the canal boats on Regent's Canal. Still enjoyable, just a different experience.

Food: If you haven't eaten properly at lunch, the market has extraordinary street food from dozens of stalls — Korean, Japanese, Ethiopian, Mexican, Caribbean, jerk chicken, pulled pork, crepes, churros. One of the best places in London for a casual afternoon feed.

Return to base: Camden Town tube → Northern Line → Leicester Square → Piccadilly Line → Green Park. About 20–25 min.

Good for: The best option for the teenagers specifically. The 14 and 15 year olds will remember Camden.

🏺 British Museum

Getting there: From Camden, tube from Camden Town → Northern Line → Tottenham Court Road, then walk 10 min, or from Primrose Hill area take a bus south. About 30–40 min from North London.

Free entry. One of the greatest museums in the world — the Egyptian collection alone justifies the visit. Allow 2–3 hours for a good visit without rushing.

Best sections for your age group:

  • Egyptian mummies — Room 62–63. The 11-year-old will love this. Real mummies, sarcophagi, canopic jars, mummified animals. Genuinely fascinating.
  • Rosetta Stone — Room 4 near the main entrance. The actual stone that cracked Egyptian hieroglyphics. Worth a stop.
  • Lewis Chessmen — medieval chess pieces, found on a Scottish island. Small but remarkable objects and the 11-year-old especially will connect with them.
  • Sutton Hoo helmet — Anglo-Saxon warrior's helmet, one of the most iconic objects in the museum. Room 41.
  • Greek antiquities — the Elgin Marbles (Parthenon sculptures) in Room 18. Monumental. Worth seeing regardless of where you stand on the repatriation debate.

Practical tip: The Great Court (central glass-roofed courtyard) is worth seeing even if you only have 20 minutes. The café there is good for a break.

Return to base: Tottenham Court Road tube → Central Line → Bond Street → Green Park. 15 min.

Good for: All three ages — the variety of the collection means everyone finds something. Best option if the weather is poor.

🌿 Hampstead Heath

Getting there: From Primrose Hill or Camden, bus or tube to Hampstead (Northern Line → Hampstead station). The Heath is a short walk from the station.

What it is: 790 acres of wild, open parkland on a hill north of London. Unlike the manicured royal parks, Hampstead Heath feels genuinely wild — ancient woodland, meadows, ponds, and hills. It's where Londoners go when they want to feel like they've escaped the city.

Parliament Hill: The highest point, with one of the best views of the London skyline anywhere — a wide panorama looking south across the entire city. On a clear day you can see Canary Wharf, the Shard, St Paul's, and the Wembley arch. Great spot for a group photo. The kite-flying here is legendary.

For the kids: The Heath has a real "adventure" quality — paths through woods, unexpected views, the feeling of being genuinely off the tourist trail. The 14 and 15 year olds tend to appreciate it more than they expect. The 11-year-old will enjoy the freedom to run around.

Kenwood House: A beautiful 18th century mansion on the northern edge of the Heath, free to enter. Houses a small but excellent art collection including a Rembrandt self-portrait. Good for a 30-min detour if anyone is interested.

Practical note: Wear comfortable shoes — the Heath has uneven ground. Late March can be muddy after rain.

Return to base: Hampstead tube → Northern Line → Leicester Square → Piccadilly → Green Park. About 25–30 min.

Good for: Best option if the weather is genuinely good. A real London experience away from the tourist circuit.

Essential Tips
Transport, bookings and things to know
Getting Around
Adults — Use Contactless
Tap your bank card or Apple/Google Pay on the yellow readers. TfL caps spend at £8.90/day (zones 1–2) automatically. Chase is TfL's official payment partner — works perfectly.
Kids (11, 14, 15) — Oyster Cards
Get Oyster cards at Heathrow T5 tube station on arrival. Ask for the Young Visitor Discount — 50% off all fares. All three kids must be present. £7/card.
Bus Hopper Fare
Changing buses within 60 minutes counts as one fare. Daily bus cap is £5.25 — unlimited buses after that. Great value.
Apple Pay Express Transit
Set up in Settings → Wallet & Apple Pay before the trip. Taps without Face ID — as fast as Oyster. Older kids can use this but won't get the 50% discount.

Good to Know
Weather in Late March
London averages 10–14°C (50–57°F). Layers and a light waterproof for everyone. The Uber Boat will be chilly even on a sunny day.
Card Clash
Keep Oyster or contactless card separate from other cards in your wallet. Two cards near the reader at once = both get charged.
Always Tap Out
Tube: tap in AND out at yellow readers. Forgetting to tap out = maximum fare charged. Buses: tap in only.
Borough Market Hours
Open Mon–Sat. Monday ~10 AM–5 PM — stalls pack up around 3 PM. Aim to arrive by 1 PM for lunch.
